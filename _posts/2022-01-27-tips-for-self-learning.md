## 3 Secrects to Self-Learning

It's no secret that I was (poorly) homeschooled growing up. I didn't learn math or science or have lessons and teachers. Every day it was up to me what to do, and most of the time I had no idea what that should be. But OH did I love to read and learning anything I could!

That passionate curiosity is what took me from a childhood without an education to one of the top CS Schools in the world. My peers these days had a lot more resources than me, parents cheering them on, amazing high school programs filled with opportunity, and wonderful experiences they've shared with me that I've learned so much from. But I have my own skills I've been able to share with them as well, one of which is a proven success in self learning. So, here's some of my top tips and tricks!

### 1. You learn more from others than you do anywhere else

Diversity is something we talk about a lot these days, but it’s true, diversity is the key to growth. I've read hundreds of books in my life, from textbooks to sci-fi, but the way I was truly able to grow my knowledge was my ability to sit and actively listen to those with wisdom to share. So many people are eager to teach if you only let them, so, let them! I have learned just as much from my 5-year-old observing butterflies as I have from a quantum physics professor. When you see people's story as a gift, you will find no shortage of opportunities to learn and grow.

### 2. Fear of failure is your biggest enemy
	
Recently I was working very hard to understand a difficult mathematics problem. I struggled with it a lot, and when midterms came, I flunked. I very excitedly went to show my friends "look! I got 8/50!" and was met with concerns for my wellbeing. One thing was very clear, we approached failure differently. See, after the exam the professor agreed to sit with me and explained in detail each of my issues. I went home with 3 new textbooks to read, and an extensive sheet of information. That half hour talk taught me more than the entire class had thus far. That midterm was very difficult, and most of the class dropped out. I stayed. I devoured the material and found myself applying new conceptual ideas from that class into my coding. My final grade came out at a solid "okay", but I wasn't concerned with my grades, I was concerned with my ability to learn, and this teacher was challenging me! To me failure is part of how we learn. Hard work is the strongest definer of my success, not talent or giftedness. Those who left the course ended up leaving because the challenge made failure a very real possibility, the same challenge which made me so excited to learn from the professor to begin with.

### 3. Be passionate

One time I read a book on art history in ancient Rome. Now, I'm interested in a lot of things, economics, mathematics, sociology, neuroscience, fabric art, and aquaponics to name a few of the recent most read topics. Turns out, I have absolutely no interest in ancient Rome. After I finished the textbook, I found I couldn't remember a single thing I read, a first for me. I felt it important to know history however, so I tried reading it again, this time taking notes. The second time while I did come out with some knowledge, I had barely absorbed it, and within a month it was all forgotten again. Unless you are excited to learn something it’s going to be so much harder to see progress in is.
